Abstract

The time it takes for humans to demonstrate a task is often greater than the amount of time a robot needs to complete that same task. Consider teaching a robot arm to pick up a cup: while the robot is capable of rapidly moving to the cup's location, a human may teach more slowly as they kinesthetically guide the arm or navigate a teleoperation interface. Instead of matching the speed of the human's demonstration, industry and other applications seek robots that imitate the task as quickly as possible. Accordingly, in this work we explore a variety of hypothesis for learning faster-than-demonstration policies. Through our experiments, we find that the most effective approach is for the robot to downsample the recorded demonstrations, and then train its policy based on this accelerated dataset. However, blindly downsampling the entire trajectory is problematic. There are parts of the motion that the robot can speed-up (e.g., moving to the cup), but there are also segments that require slow and precise motion (e.g., grasping that cup). To determine when and when not to accelerate we introduce a vision and language trajectory segmentation algorithm, VOLT. VOLT reasons over video demonstrations to mark the frames where precise motion is required: we then downsample the remaining portions of the trajectory, and provide the reformatted data to standard imitation learning methods (e.g., diffusion policies). When compared to state-of-the-art alternatives, we find that VOLT speeds-up the learned task without sacrificing performance. The key to this improvement is segmentation accuracy --- the baselines often mislabel when the robot can or cannot downsample, leading to overly conservative or incorrect policies.

Real-World Experiments

We compare VOLT against state-of-the-art imitation learning methods designed to accelerate policy execution. We evaluate each approach in a real-world setting using a Franka Emika robot arm in a tabletop setup across 4 manipulation tasks. We focus on comparing both the performance and speedup magnitude over a Normal Speed policy trained directly on the human's demonstrations. Across each task we randomize the position of the manipulated objects, and maintain the target object location fixed.

Experiment Results

Our results suggest that VOLT significantly reduces the task completion time when compared to Normal Speed policies. When compared to other methods, we find that VOLT consistently achieves the second largest speedup. Although, SAIL is the fastest, since it assign the most speed-up labels, it also negatively impacts performance, as clearly shown in the Pick and Place and Push Cup tasks. On the other hand, DemoSpeedup more frequently assigns maintain-speed labels, which results in slower policies. Overall, this results suggest that VOLT is capable of accurately determining which segments of the task can be accelerated and the segments where robots require precision.

Prompt

Full prompt utilized to segment all the demonstrations in our manuscript. 

        
VALID_SEGMENTS:
  - "speed-up"
  - "maintain-speed"

OUTPUT_EXTRACTION_PATTERN: '```json\n(.*?)\n```'

SYSTEM_PROMPT: |-
  Your are a helpful assistant that generate labels for a given video of a robot performing a task.

  Your goal is to help the robot to perform the task as fast as possible without causing 
  it to fail by segmenting the video into different substaks.
  You first need to analize the video and then localize all the substasks performed to complete 
  the main task. For each substask you need to identify
  its start and end timestamp, and classify the subtask into one of the valid segment_types: 
  ${list2string:${VALID_SEGMENTS}}.
  Your generated response must help the robot increase its task efficiency while maintaining 
  precise and critical movements.

  **Subtask Localization and Classification Guidelines:**
    - The robot should speed up whenever possible, this refers to cases that not require 
    delicate environment interactions.
    - Maintain-speed only when is necessary for the robot to work as demonstrated, mainly substasks 
    that require dexterity or precision.
    - The robot is a physical system that cannot instantly change its speed, you need to allocated 
    enough frames on slower segments when transitiong from between substasks
      that do not share the same category to allow the robot to smoothly slow down and avoid jerky motions.
    - Given direct grasping of an object the robot has enough force to securely hold it regardless of speed.
    - Fast speed will often lead to unexpected motion of objects not directly grasped by the gripper.
    - Default the events where the robot is actively avoiding obstacles without an exagerated motion, 
    i.e., the robot barely clears the obstacle as precise.
    - When the robot is done with the task and interacting with objects, the robot must quickly reach 
    is final position.

  **Generation Rules:**
    - A consise reasoning of which label is appropiate for each segments must be provided 
    prior to making a decision.
    - The list of segments must cover the entire video.
    - Only leave one frame gaps betweeen segments.
    - All robot alignment substaks must be categorized as precise.

  The following is a non-exhaustive list of common substasks:
    - Moving towards objects
    - Transporting objects
    - Avoiding obstacles
    - Grasping/Placing
    - Insertion/Retrieval
    - Pushing
    - Pouring
    - Wiping
  This is not a complete list of possible subtask, if any event not included in this list is 
  localized be sure to describe them accordingly.

  The generated response must use the 'mm:ss.ff' format for all timestamps. Final output format:
  ${OUTPUT_FORMAT}

OUTPUT_FORMAT: |-
  ```json
  [
      {
          "start_time": "mm:ss.ff",
          "end_time": "mm:ss.ff",
          "subtask": "concise substask description",
          "reasoning": "reasoning of which segment_type should be used",
          "segment_type": "${list2string:${VALID_SEGMENTS}, '/'}"
      },
      ...
  ]
  ```

USER_PROMPT: |
  Given the current task description: <|video-description|>, help the robot complete the task faster 
  than demonstrated in the video (00:00.00 - <|video-length|>)
